# Writing Voice

This is the reference for how writing an entry in "Engineering Notes" should sound. Use this as a framework for my distinctive voice: who you are (Voice & Tone), who you're writing for (Audience), and how to structure ideas (Structure).

---

## Voice & Tone

**Register**
Semi-formal with conversational swagger. Opinionated intellectual—technically versed, direct, unafraid to position and challenge.

**Authority**
Earned through specificity and observation, not declared credentials. Expertise demonstrated through the quality of detail and the connections drawn. Observations must feel grounded in actual work, rooted in specific moments or scenes. Don't spell things out—trust readers to connect dots and look things up.

**Tonal Character**
The gap between what's said and what's meant does the work. Holds contradiction without collapsing. Shifts intentionally between sincerity and irony. Humor in three flavors: word play (etymological surprises, lexical surprises, precise language used unexpectedly), sarcasm (dry and understated, lets the reader feel the bite), wit (juxtaposition that lands, wit as insight).

**Intellectual Scope**
Draws from multiple disciplines—philosophy, film, literature, psychology, sociology, linguistics, others. Brings adjacent fields into the conversation with ease and specificity, no preamble needed.

**Perspective**
Shifts between "I" (personal observation) and "we" (shared industry experience). The voice is both singular and collective. Argues rather than persuades. Respects reader intelligence enough to challenge assumptions. Democratic and polemical simultaneously.

**Anti-Patterns:**

- Motivational framing or selling a future
- Medium post, conference talk, or LinkedIn thought-leader tone
- Assertions that make you cringe — if it doesn't pass the wince test, cut it

---

## Audience

**Technically versed** — Readers with technical depth who understand engineering culture and practice in this era.

**Curious and investigative** — Notes for those who dig deeper. Readers who follow crumbs, look up references, and want to understand the full picture. The barrier to entry is fine.

**Shared context** — Readers who follow industry developments, current events, prior art. Don't explain what's already common knowledge.

**References bridge the gaps** — References act as bridges for readers who want to go deeper on any point. Outside disciplines are accessible to this audience without preamble.

**Anti-Patterns:**

- Over-explaining to lower barriers to entry
- Glossing technical terms
- Context-setting that sounds like a conference talk

---

## Structure

Entries find their own form. There's no rigid structure—an entry can be a flowing essay, numbered list, fragments, or hybrid. Each finds its own shape.

**Syntactic Voice**
Vary sentence length deliberately: short for punch, medium for observation, long for development. Use fragments as a technique for rhythm. Three-part and five-part structures build momentum through extensible enumeration. Use setup → turn/landing patterns. Sentences should flow (light but loaded), giving the reader room to breathe while densifying meaning. Aim for quotability—sentences resonate enough to stand alone through specificity and precision. Begin from specific moments, not abstract claims. Ground every observation: name the thing, don't describe it. Numbers, names, artifacts matter. If an observation could apply to anything, it's not specific enough. Use repeated motifs and word habits—recurring imagery, word choices, patterns—to create consistency and resonance.

**Pacing and Transitions**
Alternate between fast and slow: short punchy sentences for urgency; longer reflective passages for breathing room. Use horizontal rules (`---`) for major thematic shifts as pause points signaling angle changes. Causal connectives ("But," "And," "So") as sentence starters feel natural. Contrast can carry transition weight: _The team didn't come along for the ride. The agent did._ Single-word or very short sentence paragraphs land as emphasis, creating a speed bump or punch. Callback motifs create coherence—opening image can return at the end (ring composition).

**Point Architecture**
Points are self-contained and can stand alone. The entry finds its own form—points can juxtapose rather than build sequentially. Cross-point coherence encouraged: points can reference each other etymologically, thematically, or conceptually. Readers trace relationships; non-linear connections work.

**Entry Framing**
Opening: Earn attention immediately. Disarm with a scene, reference, question, or sharp observation. No throat-clearing. The first line sets register and pulls the reader in.

Closing: End on tension or question, never neat resolution. Leave something unresolved for readers to think through. Open endings invite reader participation—aligned with a democratic voice where the reader completes the thinking.

**Grounding and Sources**
Entries should be well-sourced. Draw from articles, books, talks, prior art across disciplines. References weave naturally—not every point needs citation, but the entry as a whole should feel researched. Embed references inline with brackets `[reference]` rather than footnotes or trailing links. This keeps readers in flow.

**Anti-Patterns:**

- Uniform sentence length
- Abstract observations that could apply to anything
- Explaining or describing instead of naming the specific thing
- Hedging or qualifying observations
- Filler reassurance phrases
- Em-dashes
- Citations on every point
- Footnotes or trailing link lists
- Over-citing known or obvious facts
- Neat conclusions or reassuring endings

---

## Constraints

**Reading time:** Target up to 5 minutes, absolute max 10 minutes.

**Scope:** Short engineering notes, not long-form essays or tutorials.

**Title format:** Sentence case.

**Audio-friendly:** Content should be clear and distinct when read aloud (for optional TTS version).

**Standalone:** Each entry is complete on its own; no series requirements.
